Remnants of a Planetary System Creation



In this model known as the nebular hypothesis, Most of the collapsing mass collected in the center, forming the Sun, while the rest flattened into a protoplanetary disk out of which the planets, moons, asteroids, and other small Solar System bodies formed.




There are three regions within the solar system in which millions of asteroids are concentrated.

Main Belt



Located roughly between the orbits of the planets Mars and Jupiter, About half the mass of the belt is contained in the four largest asteroids: Ceres, Vesta, Pallas, and Hygiea.

Kuiper Belt



Extending from the orbit of Neptune, is similar to the Main Belt.





Some of the Solar System's moons, such as Neptune's Triton and Saturn's Phoebe, may have originated in the region.





Pluto was discovered by Clyde Tombaugh in 1930 as the ninth planet from the Sun, after 1992, its status as a planet was questioned following the discovery of several objects of similar size in the Kuiper belt.

In 2005, Eris, a dwarf planet in the scattered disc which is more massive than Pluto, was discovered.

Oort Cloud



Predominantly icy planetesimals proposed to surround the Sun at Largest Distances, defines the cosmographical boundary of the Solar System and the extent of the Sun's Hill sphere. The outer Oort cloud is only loosely bound to the Solar System, and thus is easily affected by the gravitational pull both of passing stars and of the Milky Way itself.




These forces occasionally dislodge comets from their orbits within the cloud and send them toward the inner Solar System.
