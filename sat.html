A Magnetic World



Saturn's interior is most likely composed of a core of iron–nickel and rock, this core is surrounded by a deep layer of metallic hydrogen, an intermediate layer of liquid hydrogen and liquid helium, and finally a gaseous outer layer.





An electrical current within the metallic hydrogen layer is thought to give rise to Saturn's planetary magnetic field.





This particular combination of magnetically active elements produces unique phenomena throughout the known universe.





Like a persisting hexagonal wave pattern around the north polar vortex in the atmosphere, the sides of the hexagon are each about 13,800 km, the entire structure rotates with a period of 10hr (the same period as that of the planet's radio emissions), the pattern's origin is a matter of much speculation.

Another Planetary System



Saturn has 82 known moons, and his second-largest moon, Rhea, may have a tenuous ring system of its own, along with a tenuous atmosphere.





Traditionally, most of Saturn's moons have been named after Titans of Greek mythology.





Titan is the only satellite in the Solar System with a major atmosphere, in which a complex organic chemistry occurs.





It is the only satellite with hydrocarbon lakes.





On 6 June 2013, scientists at the IAA-CSIC reported the detection of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons in the upper atmosphere of Titan, a possible precursor for life.

Planetary Rings



The rings extend to 120,700 kilometers outward from Saturn's equator, composed predominantly of water ice.





One hypothesis is that the rings are remnants of a destroyed moon of Saturn, another hypothesis is that the rings are left over from the original nebular material from which Saturn formed, some ice in the E ring comes from the moon Enceladus's geysers.
